# Tidy Up — Friction Mechanics Menu (Curated)

A drag-any-to-any merge game needs *texture*, not a puzzle. Below: the cuts, then 10 keepers in 4 families, then a recommended v1 starter set and open questions. Every keeper passes all six pillars (zero-learning, can't-lose, chill, theme-fit, not-a-routing-puzzle, juice/helper-bonus).

## ✅ v1 DECISION (locked 2026-06-08)

**Ship THREE mechanics for v1, one per time-scale, on the new drag-any-to-any core:**
- **#1 Locked Drawer / Toy-Box** — *adjacent-merge* trigger — always-on texture (owner's seed).
- **#6 Job Ticket** — session purpose; doubles as the clients/jobs meta object.
- **#7 Fill the Shelf** — progression feel; previews the decoration meta.

**Sub-decisions:** lock language = **adjacent-merge** · **no living-home inflow in v1** (Conveyor deferred) · **board-clear is always the only win** (goals are optional gravy).

**Backlog — kept for later districts (good ideas, not v1):** #2 Buttoned Hamper (any-merge lock variant) · #3 Dust Cover · #4 Spilled Pile · #5 Tangle · #8 Clear the Floor First · #9 Tidy Conveyor · #10 Big Toys. Drip these in as content variety.

## The Keepers — 10 Mechanics in 4 Families

### Family A — Pop-Open Containers *(the "always-on" texture; the owner's seed lives here)*

**1. The Locked Drawer / Toy-Box** — A closed drawer, toy chest, or hamper sits in the tray with an item peeking out; do any merge in a touching pocket and it pops open, releasing the item.
- *How it works:* Inert container shows a silhouette of what's inside; the next merge completed in an orthogonally-adjacent pocket pops the lid; the item slides into a freed pocket and becomes normal.
- *Why it's obvious:* A closed box with something poking out universally reads "open me," and pop-on-adjacent-action is grammar every casual player already knows.
- *Friction it adds:* Light sequencing — you aim a few opening moves beside containers so their contents join the cleanup. One merge ahead, never a path to compute.
- *Helper hook:* Strong — "Master Key / Open Sesame" one-tap pops all containers; clean convenience, never required.
- *Juice:* Wooden *tok*, springy lid bounce, confetti-dust puff, item hops and settles.
- *Watch-out:* This IS the owner's seed — needs distinct per-family art (basket / chest / book-slip) so it feels themed, not a reused crate. Keep containers sparse; never ring them so a board feels pop-everything.
- **Fun: High · Complexity: Low (zero-learning safe)**

**2. The Buttoned Hamper (any-merge variant)** — Same as above but the clasp pops on the *next merge anywhere*, opening the nearest closed container in your wake.
- *How it works:* Each merge sends a sparkle-ripple that unbuttons the nearest closed hamper; a clearing run organically unbuttons them in a pleasing trickle.
- *Why it's obvious:* You never hunt for a trigger — within two merges you discover tidying itself pops the lids. Pure "you tidy, things open up" fantasy.
- *Friction it adds:* Softest possible — a warm-up-then-cascade rhythm; a couple items aren't grabbable at first.
- *Helper hook:* "Sweep / Open All" on hamper-heavy jobs; +Space when an opened item briefly crowds the tray.
- *Juice:* *plink* button pop, floppy lid bounce, item floats up on a sparkle.
- *Watch-out:* Risks being decorative since there's almost no decision — seed an occasional needed match *inside* one so opening order matters a little. Stagger pops via the CelebrationQueue or many-at-once feels noisy.
- **Fun: Medium · Complexity: Low**

> *Note:* These two are the **same gesture, two trigger styles** (adjacent-merge = more friction/intent; any-merge = pure ambient reward). Pick one as your default and use the other as a board-variety variant. Don't also ship the single-item Sticker/Knot locks at launch — one lock language is enough.

### Family B — Reveal-as-You-Clean *(fog-of-war texture; gentle discovery)*

**3. Dust Cover / Under the Pile** — Some pockets are veiled by a dust cover, cobweb, or laundry heap hiding the item's identity; a merge next to it puffs the cover off and reveals what's underneath.
- *How it works:* A greyed, lumpy silhouette sits under a soft overlay (can't be picked up). A merge completing in an adjacent pocket whooshes the cover off; the item brightens and becomes mergeable. Reveals chain pleasingly across the board.
- *Why it's obvious:* "What's under this sheet?" is instant — covers hide things, cleaning reveals them. The first puff-reveals-a-sock teaches the whole thing in one beat.
- *Friction it adds:* Light discovery/ordering — you tidy the known stuff first and let reveals surface new matches, so "the picture fills in" instead of seeing the whole solution at a glance.
- *Helper hook:* Strong — "Duster / Reveal-All" one-tap clears every cover so you can plan a big job.
- *Juice:* Soft *fwoomp* of dust motes, cover billows and dissolves, item pops in with a twinkle and chime.
- *Watch-out:* Hidden identity mildly bends "visually obvious" — keep covered items *non-essential* so a cover never strands the only match someone needed. Or pre-reveal at board-load with a brushing intro.
- **Fun: High · Complexity: Low**

**4. Spilled Pile (recede-as-you-tidy)** — A messy spill blobs across 2-3 cells, pinning items beneath; each adjacent merge sweeps a chunk away, shrinking the heap inward until everything's free.
- *How it works:* A translucent mess-blob with item silhouettes poking out; every adjacent merge whisks away the nearest chunk and frees the closest item. It only ever *recedes*, never spreads.
- *Why it's obvious:* A pile obviously wants cleaning, and watching it shrink toward where you just tidied is self-explanatory.
- *Friction it adds:* Mild attrition pacing — a multi-cell mess takes a few nearby merges to clear, so you naturally work edges inward.
- *Helper hook:* "Big Sweep / Dustpan" clears a whole pile in one tap.
- *Juice:* Each merge *shhk*s a chunk away in a dust-curl, freed items pop up brightly one by one — a tidy cascade.
- *Watch-out:* Multi-cell overlay can crowd the grid — keep piles shallow (2-3 cells) and soft-translucent so the board stays readable; shrink direction must clearly track the triggering merge.
- **Fun: High · Complexity: Low-Med**

### Family C — Bound-Together *(release-tension texture; the "untangle" beat)*

**5. Tangle / Knotted Pair** — Two or three items tied together by a visible knot won't lift; loosen it by merging (knots come undone as you tidy) and the items spring free.
- *How it works:* Bound items wiggle-but-won't-move on first touch. Best as the **any-merge "loosen-a-notch" version** (each merge anywhere loosens the knot until it springs) to *avoid* the proximity-targeting risk of the adjacent variant.
- *Why it's obvious:* A knot is the universal "stuck together, work it loose" symbol; the wiggle-on-drag teaches the constraint on first touch, wordlessly.
- *Friction it adds:* A "save the best for steady progress" beat — tangled items are content you *want* but must earn by clearing other pairs, adding anticipation and a reason not to beeline one family.
- *Helper hook:* "Detangler / Snip" one-tap pops a tangle instantly.
- *Juice:* Knot tightens-then-springs with a *boing*, string whips off, freed items do a relieved hop.
- *Watch-out:* The *adjacent* version flirts with the killed routing puzzle (constraint #5) — prefer the any-merge loosen counter, and make each notch visually loud or it feels like an arbitrary gate. Cap concurrent tangles so the board doesn't read as overwhelm. Guarantee a release always exists (can't-lose invariant).
- **Fun: High · Complexity: Low-Med** *(use the any-merge variant to stay safe)*

### Family D — Goals & Destinations *(soft pull that drips in as content; turns flat clearing into purpose)*

**6. The Job Ticket** — A little illustrated work-order names 1-3 things the client wants put away; finishing them ticks boxes and pays the headline coin lump.
- *How it works:* A diegetic card shows picture-targets ("2 folded shirts," "1 shelved book set"); producing each animates a checkmark + coin chip. Board-clear still wins; the ticket just gives a reason to prioritize.
- *Why it's obvious:* It's a picture checklist of items on your board — everyone reads a to-do list. The card *is* the instruction, drawn in-game.
- *Friction it adds:* Soft ordering — which family/tier to chase first, instead of pure-random clearing. A "work the order" rhythm with no spatial constraint.
- *Helper hook:* "Wild / Sweep" finishes a stubborn named target when you're one merge short. Never required.
- *Juice:* Stamp-thunk checkmarks, coin chip flies to the ticket, final box flips the whole card to a "JOB DONE" stamp.
- *Watch-out:* Must stay optional and warmly worded ("the client would love…") not a demand, or it reads as homework. Target mostly *mid-tier* items reachable early so it doesn't make players hoard one family. **Bonus: this fuses the board loop with the existing clients/jobs meta — same object.**
- **Fun: High · Complexity: Low**

**7. Fill the Shelf** — A themed piece of furniture beside the tray has ghosted slots; each put-away flies into the next slot, and filling it is the board's star goal.
- *How it works:* A 3-shelf bookcase / 4-cubby drawer with item-shaped holes; each top-tier put-away lands and lights a slot permanently. Filling all slots triggers a "shelf complete" glow + bonus coins.
- *Why it's obvious:* An empty shelf with item-shaped holes is the most universal "put the thing here" signal there is (every shape-sorter, every IKEA diagram). Zero text.
- *Friction it adds:* Gives flat clearing a *counted destination* — you feel put-aways accumulating, nudging you to finish whole ladders rather than leave half-merged piles.
- *Helper hook:* +Space/Sweep accelerate filling; cosmetic angle — buy nicer shelves the put-aways land in. Demand comes from *wanting* it done, not needing it.
- *Juice:* Each item arcs into its slot with a settle-wobble; the final slot sets the whole piece aglow and snaps it into the room behind the board — a mini restoration beat *inside* the level.
- *Watch-out:* Slot count must always be ≤ the board's achievable put-aways for that family, or it feels unwinnable on a can't-lose game. Keep it a slim side rail (portrait MSB budget). Make the *count* prominent or it just reads as the existing vaporize.
- **Fun: High · Complexity: Low**

**8. Clear the Floor First** — A tinted "floor/rug" zone marks the items the client cares about most; emptying that zone brightens the rug underneath as a mini before/after.
- *How it works:* A tint over normal pockets — identical drag-any-to-any rules. Goal: empty the floor zone (full-board clear still wins). As floor pockets empty, the rug under them un-dims.
- *Why it's obvious:* A highlighted area that brightens as you empty it = "clean this part." Reads like wiping a smudge.
- *Friction it adds:* Spatial-but-not-routing prioritization — *which* items to merge first (the ones on the floor), with no constraint on *how* they merge.
- *Helper hook:* "Sweep" vacuums a zone fast; the revealed rug sets up a cosmetic rug-skin store.
- *Juice:* Each emptied floor-pocket brightens with a dust-puff; the last one blooms the whole rug grimy→vivid with a contented "ahh."
- *Watch-out:* The tint must read as *priority*, not a merge restriction, or it trips the routing pillar. Two surfaces on one tray risk visual busyness — restrained art. Safer to keep full-board-clear as the win and use the floor as a "do this first" star, not a separate end.
- **Fun: Medium · Complexity: Low-Med**

### Family E — Living-Home Flow *(optional ambient inflow; "a home still being lived in")*

**9. The Tidy Conveyor (Donation Belt)** — A gentle belt on one edge carries a *finite, visible* queue of new loose items; it advances one step per merge and politely pauses when the board is full.
- *How it works:* 3-4 on-deck items advance one step per completed merge (player-paced, never timed); the lead item drops into the nearest empty pocket, or the belt waits if full. The queue is finite and visible, so the end is always in sight. Items are pre-sorted into current families so everything has a future partner.
- *Why it's obvious:* A conveyor/sushi-belt of stuff to handle is a familiar image; the visible finite queue shows when you're nearly done, no words.
- *Friction it adds:* Soft pull-rhythm — glance at what's coming, decide whether to deal with it now or finish your chain. Never blocked, just gently fed.
- *Helper hook:* "Do Not Disturb / Latch" pauses inflow; "Sort Ahead" auto-merges the next belt item; +Space relieves a full-board pause.
- *Juice:* Soft clunk-and-glide per advance; the final item rolling off and the belt clicking shut when the queue hits zero.
- *Watch-out:* **Highest chill-pillar care of the keepers.** Drip must be provably net-negative (a clear removes faster than the belt adds) and the player must *feel* the board trending empty. Must hard-stop (run dry) so every board terminates — an endless drip is a soft fail. *(The Mail Slot is the minimal version of this; ship only one — the conveyor's visible preview-queue is the richer choice.)*
- **Fun: High · Complexity: Med**

**10. Big Toys (Two-Pocket Clutter)** — Bulky items (teddy, rolled rug, toy truck) span two pockets; merging them folds down to a single-pocket item, visibly reclaiming space.
- *How it works:* Wide pieces merge like normal but the result occupies one pocket, so the merge *reclaims* room — a bigger "whew, space!" beat. Big items can only go where two contiguous pockets are free (always visible), creating gentle early space-pressure: bulky stuff goes first.
- *Why it's obvious:* A teddy is obviously bigger than a sock; of course it takes more room and frees the most when put away. You see it, no telling needed.
- *Friction it adds:* Natural opening priority ("clear the big stuff first to breathe") from glanceable occupancy — never a path puzzle.
- *Helper hook:* "+Space / Make Room" lets a big item half-sit temporarily; Shuffle re-lays the tray to open a two-pocket gap.
- *Juice:* Oversized squash-and-fold, dust puff across both pockets, the freed pocket sparkles empty.
- *Watch-out:* On *tight* boards big items could wall things off and recreate a spatial puzzle — keep them to roomy boards only. Extra wide-asset art cost per family.
- **Fun: High · Complexity: Med** *(occupancy logic is the heaviest here; validate it stays glance-able)*

## Recommended Starter Set for v1

Ship **three**, layered: one always-on texture, one drip-in content goal, one diegetic spine.

1. **The Locked Drawer / Toy-Box (#1)** — *always-on texture.* It's the owner's own seed, it's the lowest-complexity zero-learning mechanic, and it's the signature pop-juice beat that makes the board "bloom open" as you tidy. This is your default friction.
2. **The Job Ticket (#6)** — *the spine.* It costs almost no new board tech, it converts mindless clearing into "work the order," and crucially it **welds the board loop to the existing clients/jobs meta** — the same object drives both. Start here for purpose.
3. **Fill the Shelf (#7)** — *drips in as content.* A counted destination that previews the decoration meta (the room gets the furniture, now stocked) and gives put-aways a satisfying home.

**Why they combine and stay chill:** Drawer adds *moment-to-moment* texture (pop beats), Ticket adds *session-level* purpose (what to chase), Shelf adds *progression* feel (where it accumulates) — three different time-scales, none of them a clock, none reintroducing a spatial puzzle, all optional-to-ignore-and-still-win. You get layered satisfaction without any one of them turning into homework.

**Best helper-demand creators (the monetization lever):**
- **#1 Locked Drawer** → "Master Key / Open Sesame" (pop all containers) — the cleanest, most obvious helper want.
- **#3 Dust Cover / #4 Spilled Pile** → "Duster / Big Sweep" (clear all covers/piles to plan a big job) — strong demand on cluttered boards.
- **#10 Big Toys + #9 Conveyor** → "+Space / Make Room" — natural demand exactly when the tray feels briefly crowded.
Tickets and Shelves create *want-it-done* pull (Wild to finish a stubborn target) rather than need, which is the right tone for can't-lose monetization.

## Open Questions for the Owner

1. **One lock language or two?** Family A has an *adjacent-merge* trigger (more intent/friction) and an *any-merge* trigger (pure ambient reward). Pick one as the v1 default — they teach slightly different things, and shipping both lock styles *plus* tangles/stickers risks "everything's stuck to something." Which trigger feel do you want to be the game's signature?

2. **How much "living home" inflow, if any?** The Conveyor (#9) is the most distinctive, most theme-resonant idea here ("a home still being lived in") but also the only one with real chill-pillar risk. Is gentle inflow part of the v1 identity, or do we keep boards as static snapshots and add flow later once the can't-lose math is proven?

3. **Does any goal mechanic ever gate the win, or is the board-clear always the only win?** Tickets, Shelves, and Floor-zones are all designed as *bonus pull* over a full-board-clear win. Confirming "clear = win, goals are always optional gravy" keeps can't-lose airtight — but if you ever want a goal to *be* the objective, that decision changes the can't-lose invariants and should be locked now.
